Digital media: Use it wisely

c

Because of the widespread use of cell phones and other electronic devices, it would be smart to consider the real possibility that anything you say or do could be recorded and used against you at any time, whether you agreed to be recorded or not. You also should also carefully consider what you do or say on electronic media for the same reason. Once it’s there, it’s public, and even if you think it’s private, someone out there can share it with the world.

There are several examples of the lasting impact that thoughtless, misguided, inappropriate, or out-and-out stupid use of digital media has had on people’s personal and professional lives. Justine Sacco, PR director at InterActiveCorp (IAC), was headed to Africa and sent out her last tweet before her long flight without access to any digital media. The tweet was so inflammatory that, during her flight, she was trending more than major celebrities and the #hasjustinelandedyet was tweeted almost 100,000 times. When Sacco arrived, she entered a media firestorm, issued two apologies, and eventually lost her job.

Elizabeth Lauten, a staffer for a Republican congressman, resigned after she experienced backlash for using social media to mock President Obama's two children. Lauten stated that Sasha and Malia "should try 'showing a little class' and avoid dressing as though they wanted 'a spot at a bar.'" It's one thing to make fun of your own children but quite another to make fun of the president's.

Former Congressmen Anthony Weiner (D-NY) provides one of the most memorable cautionary tales of using social media. After repeatedly speaking to reporters denying he sent nude pictures of himself via Twitter to a college student, overwhelming evidence made him confront and admit to participation in the scandal, subsequently ending his political career.
